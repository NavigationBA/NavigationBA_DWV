    2. ERD Understanding¶

    (1) Cardinality Understanding¶

    (a) What is the cardinality between aircraft and flight?

    Optional one-to-many:

    • One flight can have at least zero and at most one plane assigned to it
    • One plane can be assigned to at least zero and at most many flights

    (b) What is the cardinality between ticket and flight?

    Required one-to-many:

    • One flight can have at least zero and at most many tickets
    • One ticket can have at least one and at most one flight

    (c) What is the cardinality between flight and booking?

    Optional many-to-many:

    • One flight can have at least zero and at most many bookings
    • One booking can have at least zero and at most many flights

    (d) What is the cardinality between Route and Airport?

    The relations between Route and Airport are combined with two one-to-many relationships:

    First one-to-many relationship:

    • One route can have at least one and at most one departure airport
    • One airport can be the departure airport for at least zero and at most many routes

    Second one-to-many relationship:

    • One route can have at least one and at most one arrival airport
    • One airport can be the arrival airport for at least zero and at most many routes

    (e) Which of the fields in the flight table do not allow null values?

    In the flight table, the fields Flight_Number and Date do not allow null values. These are both part of the composite primary key, and since PKs uniquely identify records, the values must be known.

    It is worth noting that Tail_Number does allow NULLs, because it is an optional FK. One flight can have at least zero and at most one plane assigned to it, so the Tail_Number can be NULL if no plane has been assigned.

    For other field, i.e., Status, we do not have enough information in the ERD about it. It may or may not allow NULLs. It is reasonable to infer that it cannot be NULL since the airline would know the status of each of the flights it operates.

    (2) Business Understanding¶

    Using the ERD above, explain whether each of the following business operations is supported by the model or not:

    (a) Can a flight be scheduled before a plane has been assigned to it?

    Yes. Since one flight can have at least zero and at most one plane assigned to it, the Tail_Number can be NULL if no plane has been assigned. This allows a flight to be scheduled before a plane has been assigned to it.

    (b) Can a flight be oversold (more tickets than seats)? Can a flight be reassigned a different plane?

    Yes, a flight can be oversold. To check whether a flight is oversold, we can count the number of tickets and compare that with the number of seats on the plane. However, simply looking at ERD, we cannot see whether there is restriction on this. So, we can infer that there is no such restriction, and a flight can be oversold.

    Yes, a flight can be reassigned a different plane. To do this, we can simply update the Tail_Number in the flight table to a different one.

    (c) Can a booking exist without any tickets?

    Yes. Since on the many side of the relationship the minimum is always 0, a booking can have at least zero and at most many tickets. Therefore, a booking can exist without any tickets. This means that a Booking_Code that never exists in the Ticket table is still a valid Booking_Code in the Booking table.

    (d) Can a ticket be issued independent of a booking?

    No. Since one ticket is associated with at least one and at most one booking, a ticket cannot be issued without being associated with a booking, and Booking_Code is a required FK in the Ticket table.

    (e) Can a booking be for more than one passenger? For more than one flight for the same passenger?

    Yes, a booking can be for more than one passenger. A booking can have more than one ticket. Each ticket can have a different Passenger_Name.

    Yes, a booking can be for more than one flight for the same passenger. A booking can have more than one ticket. Different tickets can have different Flight_Number, but the same Passenger_Name.

    (f) Can a ticket be booked with an open date (i.e. known flight, but date chosen later)

    No. One ticket is associated with at least one and at most one flight, and Date is part of the composite PK of the flight table, i.e., Date is a required FK in the Ticket table. Therefore, a ticket cannot be booked with an open date.

    (g) Can a person join the rewards program before making a booking? Must all passengers be rewards members?

    Yes, a person can join the rewards program before making a booking. Since one ticket can have at least zero and at most one rewards member, a ticket can be associated with zero rewards members, i.e., Rewards_Member is an optional FK in the Ticket table. Therefore, a person can join the rewards program before making a booking.

    No, all passengers do not have to be rewards members. Since Rewards_Member is an optional FK in Ticket, therefore it can be NULL, which means that a passenger can be a non-rewards member. Therefore, not all passengers have to be rewards members.

    3. SQL Queries Interpretation¶

    What is the business intent behind, and usefulness of the data returned, by each of the following queries?

    (a)

    SELECT *
FROM Flight
WHERE Tail_Number IS NULL
  AND Date BETWEEN '2025-03-25' AND '2025-03-31' -- or any future week

    This query finds all flights that are scheduled to operate in the future week but do not have a plane assigned to them yet.

    This can be useful for the airline to identify flights that may need attention in terms of plane assignment, and to ensure that all flights have planes assigned before their scheduled departure dates.

    (b)

    SELECT
  Flight_Number,
   Date,
   COUNT(*) / c.Seats as Rate
FROM Flight f
  INNER JOIN Ticket t
    ON t.Flight_Number = f.Flight_Number
      AND t.Date = f.Date
  INNER JOIN Aircraft c
    ON c.Tail_Number = f.Tail_Number
WHERE Status = 'Scheduled'
GROUP BY f.Flight_Number, f.Date, c.Seats
HAVING Rate > 0.95
  OR Rate < 0.50
ORDER BY f.Date

    This query calculates the booking rate for each scheduled flight by number of tickets sold divided by the total number of seats on the aircraft. It then filters for flights that are either over 95% booked or under 50% booked.

    This information can be useful for identifying flights that are either very popular (overbooked) or not popular (underbooked). For overbooked flights, we can consider re-assigning a larger plane or planning more flights on the same route. For underbooked flights, we can consider re-assigning a smaller plane or merging flights on the same route to improve efficiency.
