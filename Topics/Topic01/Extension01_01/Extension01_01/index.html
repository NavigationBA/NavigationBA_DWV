    Topic 1 Extension 1 - Evidence-Based Management¶

    1. Concepts of Evidence-Based Management¶

    (1) Core Idea of Evidence-Based Management¶

    Evidence-Based Practice (EBP) is about making better-quality decisions:

    Good decisions should be based on:

    • Critical thinking
    • The best available evidence

    Most managers do use evidence in decision-making, but they often pay too little attention to the quality of that evidence

    • As a result, decisions are often based on:

      • unfounded beliefs
      • management fads
      • popular ideas promoted by management gurus

    • Relying on weak or low-quality evidence can lead to:

      • bad decisions
      • poor outcomes
      • limited understanding of why things go wrong

    Evidence-Based Practice aims to improve the way decisions are made. It helps practitioners:

    • evaluate how much they can trust the evidence they already have
    • identify and search for additional relevant evidence
    • use evidence more systematically in everyday work

    (2) Definition & Steps of Evidence-Based Management¶

    Evidence-based management is about making decisions through the conscientious, explicit and judicious use of the best available evidence from multiple sources.

    Evidence-Based Management: Six-A Framework: Six steps to increase the likelihood of a favorable outcome:

    • Ask: Translate a practical issue or problem into an answerable question
    • Acquire: Systematically search for and retrieve evidence
    • Appraise: Critically judge the trustworthiness and relevance of the evidence
    • Aggregate: Weigh and pull together the evidence
    • Apply: Incorporate the evidence into to the decision-making process
    • Assess: Evaluate the outcome of the decision taken

    Example of Applying the Six-A Framework: High Employee Turnover

    Analysing high employee turnover in a company using the Six-A Framework:

    • Ask: What are the causes of high employee turnover in our company?

    • Acquire: Search for relevant data and information, e.g.,

      • exit interviews
      • employee surveys
      • industry benchmarks

    • Appraise: Evaluate the quality and relevance of the evidence, e.g.,

      • Are the exit interviews conducted systematically?
      • Are the employee surveys representative?
      • Are the industry benchmarks comparable?

    • Aggregate: Synthesize the evidence to identify common themes and patterns, e.g.,

      • Are there recurring reasons for leaving?
      • Are there specific departments or roles with higher turnover?
      • Are there any correlations between turnover and factors like compensation, work environment, or career development opportunities?

    • Apply: Develop and implement strategies to address the identified causes, e.g.,

      • Improve employee engagement
      • Enhance compensation and benefits
      • Provide career development opportunities

    • Assess: Monitor the impact of the implemented strategies on employee turnover rates, e.g.,

      • Track turnover rates over time
      • Conduct follow-up surveys
      • Gather feedback from employees to evaluate the effectiveness of the interventions

    (3) Definition of Evidence¶

    In Evidence-Based Practice, evidence basically means information:

    • Evidence is not limited to numbers or statistics.

    • Evidence can be:

      • quantitative (numbers, metrics, statistics)
      • qualitative (descriptions, observations, experiences)

    Evidence can come from different types of information, such as:

    1. Scientific research

      • Published studies and academic findings

      • Helps identify generally applicable facts about:

        • People
        • Organizations
        • Management practices

    2. Organizational or business data

      • Internal company information

      • For example:

        • Performance metrics
        • Financial indicators
        • Operational observations
        • Business conditions

    3. Professional experience

      • The experience and judgment of practitioners can also count as evidence
      • An entrepreneur’s previous experience building different businesses may provide useful insight into what is likely to work

    Key Principle of Evidence-Based Practice:

    • Any type of evidence can be used

      • Management decisions should be based on different forms of evidence
      • Not just one source

    • But it should only be included if it is:

      • Trustworthy
      • Relevant

    (4) Reasons for Using Evidence-Based Management¶

    Management based on experiences and judgment:

    • Most management decisions are not based on the best available evidence

    • Instead, many practitioners make decisions mainly based on:

      • personal experience
      • personal judgment

    Three problems with this approach:

    1. Personal Judgment Alone Is Not Enough

      • Personal judgment is often not a reliable source of evidence, and experience alone does not automatically lead to good decisions

      • This is because people are vulnerable to:

        • systematic errors
        • cognitive biases
        • information-processing limitations

      • As a result:

        • Decision quality can be poor
        • Even experienced professionals can make weak forecasts or bad judgments

      • Examples of areas where human judgment can be weak:

        • credit ratings
        • economic forecasting
        • political developments
        • medical diagnoses

    2. Blindly Copying “Best Practices”

      • Practitioners often copy what other organizations are doing

      • This usually happens through:

        • benchmarking
        • so-called “best practices”

      • This is risky because:

        • A practice that works in one organization may not work in another

        • Different organizations have:

          • different contexts
          • different cultures
          • different goals
          • different constraints

      • Important point: Benchmarking can provide ideas, but it is not proof that something will work in your own situation

    3. Barriers to Evidence-Based Practice

      • Lack of training: Many practitioners have not been trained to:

        • evaluate evidence critically
        • judge trustworthiness and relevance

      • Poor access to organizational information: Important data may be:

        • hard to access
        • incomplete
        • low quality

      • Lack of awareness of scientific evidence

        • Many practitioners are not familiar with current research

        • This creates a gap between:

          • what managers believe works
          • what research actually shows works

    Research on Evidence-Based Management

    Survey findings:

    • A survey of 950 American HR practitioners found a large gap between:

      • what practitioners think is effective
      • what scientific research shows is effective

    • Similar findings were reported in other countries

    Meaning of this finding

    • Many managers rely too much on:

      • personal experience
      • “best practices”
      • leadership beliefs

    • And too little on:

      • scientific evidence
      • organizational evidence

    Consequences of not using evidence-based practice

    • Organizations may spend huge amounts of money on management practices that are:

      • ineffective
      • or even harmful

    • This can negatively affect:

      • organizations
      • employees
      • clients

    Case Example: Importance of Evidence-Based Practice

    Situation:

    • An American IT company believed for years that technical expertise was the most important quality of a good manager

    • The company assumed that the best managers were those who:

      • Let employees work independently
      • Only stepped in when technical problems occurred

    • The company found that when employees were asked what they valued most in a manager:

      • Technical expertise ranked last

      • Employees valued other qualities more, such as:

        • Asking good questions
        • Taking time to meet
        • Caring about employees’ careers and lives

    This shows that managers with these people-oriented qualities had:

    • Top-performing teams
    • Happier employees
    • Lower staff turnover

    Main Lesson from the Case

    • The company relied too long on an assumption
    • But scientific evidence had already shown that supportive, communicative managers are more effective
    • If the company had used evidence earlier, it could have improved management practices much sooner

    2. Sources of Evidence in Management Decision-Making¶

    Decision-makers should look for evidence from multiple sources:

    • Scientific evidence: Findings from published scientific research
    • Organizational evidence: Data, facts and figures gathered from the organization
    • Experiential evidence: The professional experience and judgment of practitioners
    • Stakeholder evidence: The values and concerns of people who may be affected by the decision

    (1) Scientific Evidence¶

    Scientific evidence refers to findings from published scientific research, especially studies published in academic journals

    • Management research is growing rapidly.

    • There is also useful research from outside management, such as:

      • psychology
      • communication
      • decision science
      • organizational behavior

    Scientific evidence is important because:

    • Scientific research provides evidence about:

      • what tends to work
      • what does not work
      • under what conditions something is effective

    • It is useful because many management problems are not unique.

    • Similar issues often appear across:

      • organizations
      • industries
      • countries
      • professional settings

    To use scientific evidence well, practitioners need to:

    • know how to search for relevant studies

    • be able to judge:

      • trustworthiness
      • relevance
      • applicability to their own context

    Case Example – Scientific Evidence

    Situation:

    • A large Canadian law firm planned to merge with a smaller nearby firm.

    • The goal was to integrate only the back office:

      • IT
      • Finance
      • Facilities

    • The legal practices and front office would remain separate.

    • Partners believed that cultural differences between the two firms might create serious problems.

    What Scientific Evidence Showed:

    • A meta-analysis of 46 studies with a combined sample of 10,710 mergers and acquisitions was found.

    • The study showed:

      • Cultural differences can hurt merger effectiveness
      • But mainly when the intended level of integration is high

    Key Insight: In mergers with low integration, cultural differences may actually be associated with benefits.

    (2) Organizational Evidence¶

    Organizational evidence refers to data, facts, and figures gathered from the organization itself.

    Possible Forms of Organizational Evidence

    • Hard data

      • Cash flow
      • Costs
      • Return on investment (ROI)
      • Market share
      • Customer satisfaction scores
      • Repeat business
      • Product return rates
      • Staff turnover
      • Productivity levels
      • Medical errors

    • Soft data

      • Employee perceptions
      • Attitudes toward senior management
      • Organizational culture
      • Job satisfaction

    Organizational evidence is important because it is essential for:

    • identifying problems
    • understanding likely causes
    • evaluating possible solutions
    • deciding what is needed for implementation

    Case Example – Organizational Evidence

    Situation:

    • A large insurance company planned to change its structure - from regional to product-based

    • Management Assumption: The board believed restructuring would:

      • Improve market presence
      • Increase customer focus

    • Concern from Sales Managers: Sales managers argued that removing the regional structure would:

      • Weaken customer relationships
      • Reduce customer service quality

    What Organizational Evidence Showed: Internal analysis found:

    • Customer satisfaction was already above industry average

    • There was a strong negative correlation between:

      • Account managers’ travel expenses
      • Customer satisfaction

    Interpretation:

    • Sales managers who lived closer to customers had higher customer satisfaction
    • The board decided to keep the regional structure

    (3) Experiential Evidence¶

    Experiential evidence refers to the professional experience and judgment of practitioners, which includes:

    • Managers
    • Consultants
    • Business leaders
    • Experienced professionals

    Experiential evidence is not just intuition, opinion, or belief. It is based on "Tacit knowledge":

    • Repeated experience
    • Reflection on outcomes
    • Lessons learned over time

    Professional experience is important because it can help practitioners judge:

    • whether an issue really needs attention
    • whether internal data are trustworthy
    • whether research findings fit the situation
    • whether a proposed solution is likely to work in practice

    Case Example – Experiential Evidence

    Situation:

    • A university hospital asked nurses to prepare personal development plans

    • These plans focused on:

      • Aspirations
      • Career priorities

    • HR Director’s Argument:

      • The HR director used Maslow’s hierarchy of needs

      • He argued that nurses in the emergency department should be excluded because:

        • They were facing serious safety hazards
        • Basic needs should come before professional development

    • What Experienced Practitioners Said:

      • Experienced managers and nurses disagreed
      • Their professional experience suggested that nurses in difficult environments are often more interested in development and self-improvement

    Additional Evidence:

    • Scientific database searches found:
    • No strong empirical support for Maslow’s theory in this context

    Conclusion:

    • The nurses’ view was accepted
    • The program was not restricted in the way the HR director proposed

    (4) Stakeholder Evidence¶

    Stakeholder evidence refers to the values and concerns of people who may be affected by a decision.

    Stakeholders includes:

    • Internal stakeholders

      • Employees
      • Managers
      • Board members

    • External stakeholders

      • Suppliers
      • Customers
      • Shareholders
      • Government
      • The public

    Stakeholder evidence is important because it helps decision-makers understand:

    • What different groups consider important
    • How people are likely to react to decisions
    • Whether a decision is likely to be accepted or resisted

    Examples of Stakeholder Priorities: Different stakeholders may care more about:

    • Short-term gain vs long-term sustainability
    • Employee well-being vs employee output
    • Organizational reputation vs profitability
    • Participation vs top-down control

    Case Example – Stakeholder Evidence

    Situation:

    • A telecommunications company conducted a survey on employee satisfaction with supervisors

      • The survey was sent to 12,500 employees

      • It included demographic questions such as:

        • postcode
        • date of birth
        • job title

    • Problem:

      • Only 582 employees responded
      • Response rate was less than 5%

    What Stakeholder Evidence Showed:

    • A focus group was held with employees

    • Employees explained that they were worried demographic data could:

      • reveal their identity
      • make the survey not truly anonymous

    What Was Changed: The company:

    • removed postcode
    • replaced date of birth with an age range

    Result & Conclusion:

    • The revised survey achieved a 67% response rate

    • Understanding stakeholder concerns helped improve:

      • Participation
      • Trust
      • The usefulness of the data collected

    3. Assessing the Quality of Evidence¶

    (1) Critically Appraising Evidence¶

    Critical appraisal means assessing whether evidence is:

    • Trustworthy
    • Relevant
    • Sufficient
    • Free from serious bias

    Reasons for Critical Appraisal of Evidence:

    • Evidence is never perfect. It can be:

      • Incomplete
      • Misleading
      • Exaggerated
      • Based on weak information

    • Sometimes evidence looks strong, but is actually weak. For example:

      • A strong claim may be based on:

        • only one piece of information
        • a single unreliable source

      • A colleague’s confident opinion may be based on:

        • only an anecdote
        • limited personal experience

      • A long-used organizational practice may:

        • never have been properly tested or evaluated
        • be based on outdated assumptions

    • Therefore, not all evidence should be accepted at face value, and evidence must be carefully and systematically evaluated.

    Key Questions of Evidence Appraisal:

    • Where did the evidence come from?
    • How was the evidence gathered?
    • Is it the best available evidence?
    • Is there enough evidence to support a conclusion?
    • Could the evidence be biased?

    Appraising Different Types of Evidence

    1. Personal / Experiential Evidence

      • How many times has this person faced this issue?
      • Were the situations similar to the current one?
      • Is the experience broad enough to be useful?

    2. Organizational Evidence

      • How were the figures calculated?
      • Are the numbers accurate?
      • Are they reliable?
      • Are they complete and meaningful?

    3. Scientific Evidence

      • How was the study designed?
      • How were the data collected?
      • How was the outcome measured?
      • Are there alternative explanations for the result?

    (2) Focusing on the Best Available Evidence¶

    Focusing on the best available evidence means:

    • In most situations, there is more than one type of evidence available
    • The challenge is not just collecting evidence, but deciding which evidence should we trust the most?
    • The quality of decisions improves when we use the most trustworthy evidence available
    • In other words: Better evidence makes better decisions

    Reasons for Focusing on the Best Available Evidence:

    • Different sources of evidence are not equally convincing

    • Some evidence is:

      • weak
      • limited
      • uncertain

    • Other evidence is:

      • stronger
      • more systematic
      • more trustworthy

    Examples of stronger vs weaker evidence:

    • If you want to know which holiday destination in Ireland has the least chance of rain in early August:

      • The most trustworthy evidence would be rainfall statistics, not a colleague’s personal memory from visiting once
      • Systematic data is usually more trustworthy than isolated personal experience

    • If a hospital wants to know whether Six Sigma can reduce medical errors:

      • Stronger evidence would come from a study of 150 European university hospitals measuring medical errors before and after implementation
      • This would be more trustworthy than one colleague’s experience in a single small hospital

    However, sometimes the ideal high-quality evidence does not exist

    • For some decisions:

      • there may be no strong scientific evidence
      • there may be little or no organizational data

    • When strong evidence is missing, decision-makers may need to rely on:

      • professional experience
      • case studies
      • pilot testing
      • trial-and-error learning

    • However:

      • This evidence should still be used carefully
      • Its limitations should be recognized

    4. Common Misunderstandings about Evidence-Based Practice¶

    Misconceptions are a major barrier to adopting evidence-based practice (EBP)

    Misconceptions usually come from:

    • Limited understanding
    • Oversimplified views of EBP

    (1) Misconception 1: EBP ignores professional experience¶

    Misunderstanding: EBP only values research and ignores practitioners’ experience

    Reality: EBP uses multiple sources of evidence, including experiential evidence (professional experience)

    Key Points

    • No single type of evidence is always superior

    • Professional experience is valuable when:

    • it is critically appraised

    • it is relevant and trustworthy

    • Experience helps:

      • interpret other types of evidence
      • adapt decisions to specific contexts

    Important conclusion about experience:

    • Deep specialized experience is more reliable
    • Unrelated or shallow experience is less reliable

    (2) Misconception 2: EBP is all about numbers and statistics¶

    Misunderstanding: EBP = only quantitative data and statistics

    Reality: EBP includes:

    • qualitative evidence
    • experience
    • stakeholder input
    • not just numbers

    Key Points

    • You do not need to be a statistician
    • But you should understand basic concepts:
    • sample size
    • confidence intervals
    • effect sizes

    • statistical vs practical significance

    • EBP is about statistical thinking, not doing complex statistics

    (3) Misconception 3: Managers don’t have time for EBP¶

    Misunderstanding: Decisions must be made quickly, so EBP is impractical

    Reality:

    • EBP often involves:

      • Preparation before decisions
      • Reflecting on evidence quality

    • Most management decisions:

    • are not instant

    • take weeks or months

    Key Points:

    • Even fast decisions require reliable evidence

    • Good decision-makers:

      • anticipate what evidence will be needed
      • prepare in advance

    • Time is not usually a barrier - EBP can actually improve both speed and quality of decisions

    (4) Misconception 4: Every organization is unique, so research doesn’t apply¶

    Misunderstanding: Our organization is different, so scientific evidence is useless

    Reality: Organizations are not identical, but not completely unique either

    Key Points

    • Many management problems are repeated across organizations

    • Organizations often respond in similar ways

    • Good practitioners adapt general evidence to their specific context, rather than rejecting it completely

    (5) Misconception 5: Without high-quality evidence, nothing can be done¶

    Misunderstanding: If strong evidence is not available, decision-making must stop

    Reality:

    • Sometimes, high-quality evidence does not exist

    • In such cases, practitioners should:

      • use the best available evidence
      • even if it is limited

    Key Approach

    • Use pilot testing, experimentation, learning by doing, etc.
    • Treat actions as prototypes
    • Continuously test, evaluate, and improve

    (6) Misconception 6: Good evidence gives you the answer¶

    Misunderstanding: Evidence directly tells you what decision to make

    Reality: Evidence is not an answer - It does not speak for itself

    Key Points:

    • Evidence requires:

      • Interpretation
      • Context
      • Critical thinking

    • For example, A score (e.g., 10 points) is meaningless without knowing the average or understanding the context

    • Evidence is

      • not conclusive
      • not absolute truth
      • often uncertain

    • EBP helps you make better-informed decisions but does not guarantee a perfect answer
